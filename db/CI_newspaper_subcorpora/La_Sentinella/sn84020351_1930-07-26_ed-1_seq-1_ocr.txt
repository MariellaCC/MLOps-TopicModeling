THE VALUE OF ALERTNESS
By Dr. Joseph Pantaleone
Life is full of pitfalls, dangers and temptations at every turn
of the game ; it is also full of opportunities and infinite possibilities
for each one of us at every step if we only had the eyes to see
them. It takes a constantly alert mind to play the game at its
best. Let us analyze a little this exceedingly valuable quality
which for want of a better word I have called alertness.
The alert man avoids those temptations and momentary
pleasures which may extort a great price in the future. and involve
very much suffering. He is truly a master of the art of weighing
pleasures. The alert man is determined to get a firm grip on him
self and make himself do things he may not like for the sake of
the great future advantages he may derive from these moment
ary sacrifices. So alertness means self-mastery. It involves effort,
work, watchfulness but in the end it repays itself a thousand fold
in the joys and satisfactions it yields.
Most men of real wisdom throughout the ages have been
advocates of strenuous life for only in hard work are to be found
the most complete satisfactions and deepest human joys, and part
of the program of the strenuous life is the policy of. constant
alertness, keen awareness of all that is going on around us in the
world and all that has gone' on in the past as well as the will and
understanding to derive the best advantages from the circum
stances of lifé as they face us at the present moment.
Alertness means being fully conscious of life's great truths
and life's mysterious and miraculous nature. Our lives are too
short and if we are to make the most magnificent masterpieces
possible out of them we cannot afford, to sleepon the job one
minute our task is too great we must be ever on the go,
ever planning ana looking forward with wise forèsight and ever
carrying on our present tasks in the most efficient manner
possible. The more we have of alertness the more we develop the
God within us the less alert we are, the less God-like and closer
to the animal we are for then to live is merely to vegetate.
The alert man keeps up to date is as tactful as possible" in all
his reaction with his fellows, uses his time wisely exhibits real
judgment and does the right thing at the right time. Sometimes
however, to be alert may mean to go against- the crowd for the
discerning man sees far ahead of his fellows and sees possibilities
in new ideas which it takes ages for the rest of the world to'catch
up to. So, frequently he stands alone and has thecourage to voice
his opinions against the whole world of " such stuff are the
world's greatest heroes made and there are many other qualities
which the truly alert man must possess.
But suffice it to remember that in an age like ours when
competition in all fields is so sharp a mental state of constant
watchfuSiess and keen alertness is absolutely essential if we are
to keep ahead of th other fellow in all our activities.

bright new bathing suit. Beside her appeared a young man. 'Til
get your suit wet for you", he said gaily and he shoved her into

COLLARS
One of the first things a man does when he settles down to
his paper in the evening it to take off his collar and hang it on
the back of his chair. Then for the first time in the day- he is
free from the bond of uniformity. For all the articles of male
dress, except shoe laces, the collar is one in which no man can
exercise his individuality. His neck may be thick or lean, long or
short, wrinkled or smooth, but once inside a collar his neck is no
longer his neck; it is simply neck. And as neck, man is always
conscious of it, conscious of that constant, irritating pressure of
firm linen, that stricture to the flow of eloquence, that torture to
the windpipe and vocal cords.
So when a man goes home to his wife, he takes his collar off.
