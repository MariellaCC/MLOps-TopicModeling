LETTER POSTAGE
There has been some talk at Washington that an increase in
the rate of letter postage may be advocated, with the idea of
raisinsr the rate from two to two and half or three cents. Such a
' plan would hit every family, and
It may be remarked that as the department needs more reve
nue, it may seem necessary either to raise letter postage or put it
on parcel post. Also that an increased rate on parcel post would
get the government in bad with the farmers, who use this service
constantly in buying goods through mail orders.
The mail order business is a form of trade that creates comp
etition in thousands of towns and villages, for the element that
does the most to promote community development, the local
merchants. If organization work has to be done in any community,
if committees have to be appointed and give a lot of time to get
things done, if money has to be raised, it is commonly the mer
chants of the community who do the bulk of the work. Every
thing possible should be done to make it " easy for them to do
business.
If the government is carrying its mail order business for rates
lower that the cost of transporting these packages, then this form
of service would seem to provide a kind of subsidy to the mail
order trade, and it does not seem good policy to give any such
favor. - , .
The two cent rate for carrying letters has commonly been said
to be a profitable one," since letters take up very little space, and
a lot of them can be carried in very small compass. There seems
no reason for charging an added profit on them, if the parcel post
business is being transported at a loss. This will be the sentiment
of the businessman anyway.

AUTOMOBILE LEARNERS
Almost any good day on the roads you can see people learning
;o drive automobiles. Every year there is a new crop of learners,
nd one approaches them with some fear, never knowing what
hey may do.
And yet they do not apparently cause any more accidents than
those bold and over confident drivers who have escaped trouble
io far, and who feel a glorious confidence that they will always be
able to avoid it, no matter what they do. The beginners at least
re keenly anxious to make good, and are putting every bit of
their brain power to work on the problem of learning. As long
as they have that attitude, they will not cause any mishaps.

with a group of newspaper men early one mornig, after "business"
had slackened a bit. "Do you know," he. mused, "after all the
years I have spent as a cop, I don't believe I have seen a dozen
tormal young men who knew some trade thoroughly ever even
accused of crime. It's the young drifters, the hanger-around the
corners, the "drug store cowboys," as they call them, that get into
trouble. The steady young feller who plugs through an apprentice
ship and learns his. trade is hardly ever a stick-up man, thug, or
anything of that kind."
And the old lieutenant has the right of it. There is a steady
discipline about the serving of an apprenticeship in a trade that
makes for character. The craftsman has a pride, a self-reliance,
a confidence in himself that is a sobering intluence. The drifter,
the boy who work here a while, there a couple of months,, who
slides around , loafing a good deal, gambling a good deal, hang
ing around back rooms , of pool parlors and speakeasies, getting
pretty near to the crime-line many times and feeling that it-is
clever to "put one over" on the police, he is the potential gunman,
the candidate for high dishonor as burglar, stickup man, or other
unlovely callings.

OPEN THE DOORS
Every year a large number of persons are fatally poisoned by
carbon monoxide given off by the exhaust gases of automobiles,
nnd many others escape the same fate only because they are
discovered (often in an unconscious condition), removed from the
dangerous atmosphere, and treated promptly and intelligently.
Although these cases uccor throughout the year, they are more
numerous during the winter months because of the unnecessary
.:;nd extremely dangerous (but unfortunately quite common,)
practice of "warming up" automobile engines in small garages,
with all doors and windows closed.
There is no difficulty in preventing these fatalities all that
is necessary is to open the doors and windows of the garage before
starting- the engine, and leaving them open. The fresh air entering
from outiside will prevent a dangerous concentration of carbon
monoxide.
Occasionally, also, person, afe overcome by carbon nonoxide
while riding in closed automobiles, and while sitting in stationary
closed cars with the engines running. In such cases the gas
usually comes from a defective car heater or a leaky exhaust pipe.
The remedy is simple always have one or more of the 'car
windows open, Remember it better to be alive and chilly, than
dead and cold.
