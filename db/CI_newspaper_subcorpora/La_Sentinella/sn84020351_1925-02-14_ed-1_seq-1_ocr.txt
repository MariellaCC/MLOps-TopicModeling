Am Italian Renaissance in America
For many years we have had in America permanent and
efficiently organized bureaus of French culture, of German cul
ture, of Spanish culture, and of other foreign cultures. These
organizations have not only succeeded in making known in this
country the intrinsic merits of their respective intellectual con
tributions, but have contributed in interesting the American
public toward a more sympathetic view of their economic, politi
cal and international problems.
But what do we find has been done for Italy along these
lines? It will not be necessary for me, I know, to convince the
general reader that up until very recently our efforts in the prop
agation of Italian culture have been comparatively negligible.
The past few years, however, have shown a remarkable
growth and revival of interest throughout the United States in
the study of the Italian language and literature, and today we
find Italian being taught in institutions wheretofore it was en
tirely unknown. For many years we have known Italy to attract
tourists and scholars through its immeasurable facilities for
studying ancient culture and the fine arts. If the genius and ac
complishments of its native sons were not sufficient to draw
people to the Italian peninsula, nature itself would provide em
bellishments of a kind to delight the weary traveler and provide
solace and comfort for its restless soul.
But, furthermore, and what is even more interesting, since
the advent of the Great War, Italy has attracted universal atten
tion through the remarkable rapidity of its economic, social
and scientific reconstruction. As I have before stated in the in
vestigation of nearly every branch of natural and social science
in Zoology, in Anatomy, and especially in Geometry and Mathe
matical Physics, American research workers have discovered
that Italians occupy some of the foremost positions of the world.
The Geological Society of America has recently recommended
the study of Italian as a great asset in geological investigation's.
Italy is therefore making its influence felt, not only through the
contemporary workers of such literary figures as Croce, D'An-
nunzio, Papini, Pirandello, Prezzolini, but also through the un
questioned scientific prestige and acknowledged supremacy of
men who until recently were either unknown, or generally ne
glected men like Senator Volterra of Rome, the world's great
est living mathematician ; men like Boggiu, xTIarconi, Emery,
Monticelli, etc.
The need of establishing a permanent Centre of Italian Cul
ture in America to serve as a bureau of intellectual liaison be
tween the two countries and to acquaint Americans not only with
the cultural achievements of Ancient and Renaissance Italy, but
of modern and contemporary Italy as well, has long been felt.
The present situation, however, reveals that this need has never
been more urgent. It was for this reason that there was formed
in the spring of 1923 the Iastitute of Italian Culture in the United
States, under the auspices of Columbia University, in coopera
tion with the Institute of International Education, the Italy
American Society, the Order of the Sons of Italy, the American
Association of Teachers of Italian, the Italian Teachers Associa
tion, and the Institute of Arts and Sciences. Because of his long
interest both as a scholar and teacher in Italian culture, and of
his persistent efforts to promote cultural relations between Amer
ica and Italy, Professor J. L. Gerig, executive officer of the De
partment of Romance Languages, Columbia University, was
elected president of the Institute, and through his kind offices
temporary headquarters were established in Philosophy Hall of
Columbia "University. During its first year of existence, the In
stitute tried1 to cope with the many demands made upon it, but
found its resources and facilities inadequate. It was then decided
to follow the suggestion of Dr. Nicholas Murray Butler, Presi
dent of Columbia University, to find ways and means to pur
chase, equip, and establish an Italian House to serve as the na
tional bureau of intellectual relations between Italy and America.
Judge John J. Freschi, a pioneer and an unselfish devotee of the
Italian cause in America, was chosen Chairman of the National
Executive Committee to organize a drive to solicit funds for the
House. The program of the Italian House fund, under the aus
pices of the Institute of Italian Culture in the United States, was
formally launched at a dinner held recently at The Faculty Club
of Columbia University, where many Italians and Americans,
prominent in their respective fields of endeavor, were present.
Thanks to the unselfish devotion and the untiring efforts of
its inspiring leadefs, Judge Freschi and Dr. Gerig, the drive has
had a most auspicious beginning. Certainly the cause )f Italy
has never had a greater manifestation of that rare spirit of co
ordination and cooperation so essential to the success of any
great undertaking.
It is indeed inspiring to know that Italians and Italian or
ganizations from all quarters of the country are rallying to the
aid of the National Executive Committee. Not only have such
well-known organizations as the Institute of International Edu
cation, the Order of the Sons of Italy, the Italian Teachers'
Association, the American Association of Teachers of Italian, the
ex-Combattente', -the Physicians, etc., offered to cooperate, but
we have received enthusiastic resolutions of endorsement from
the various college circoli, such as the Italian Intercollegiate
Association, the Circolo Dante Alighieri of City College, the Cir
colo Mazzini of New York University, the Circolo of Hunter
College and Fordham University, and many of them have al
ready sent in their contributions to the Treasurer, Chev. G. Gir
ardon at No. 39'J Broadway.
An example of how this movement has taken root among the
various organizations is furnished by the following extract from
a letter addressed to Dr. Gerig by Mr. Vincent Ciota on behalf of
the Circolo Dante Alighieri of the College of the City of New
York :
"Doctor Gerig, you have 'fired the shot heard around the
world', and you will undoubtedly realize your magnificent aims.
We, students, are with you and we will keep our shoulders to
the wheel until this noble cause has reached its zenith, yea perpetually."
What is even more unusual and at the same time most in
spiring, we have in addition, just learned that the French Cul
tural Organization, known as the Institut des Etude Francaise, at
Columbia, through the kind efforts of its director, Professor
Philippe de la Rochelle, has not only passed a resolution express
ing its hearty endorsement of the Italian House Drive, but its
members have pledged to donate the proceeds of their annual
play this year to the Casa Italiana Fund. Furthermore, Dr. Fede
rico de Onis and Dr. Frank Calcitt, directors of the Instituto de
Las Espanos, one of the most influential Spanish Cultural Socie
ties in America, have also communicated to us the fact that their
organization heartily approves the aims of the Italian Institute
and will start a subscription campaign for our benefit.
Certainly this drive in behalf of Italian Culture is sowing
seeds of a most fruitful and beiieficial kind. Such whole-hearted
expressions of cooperation and support among the different lan
guage groups, not only serves to spur us on to greater efforts
but they argue well for a spontaneous and genuine spirit of in
ternational amity and good feeling.
The work of the National Executive Committee has already
borne such fruition that Judge Freschi now has branch commit
tees organized in states as far distant as Louisiana, Maryland,
Illinois, Wisconsin and California. Under the leadership of
Giuseppe Prezzolini, the distinguished author and critic, a for
eign committee has been formed with headquarters at Rome,
Italy ; and Charles H. Tutt, who recently was awardecfthe fellow
ship of the Order of Sons of Italy for a year's study abroad, is
organizing student committees in the various Italian Universi
ties. The Italian Press has been giving the movement splendid
publicity, and the cooperation that has been offered by such
well-known editors as Luigi Barzini in the Corriere d'America
and Agostino de Biasi in the Carroccio is especially grateful.
The American Press and Americans in general, have not
only expressed their special commendation of the proposed drive
for an Italian House, but many well-known men have accepted
membership in the National Executive Committee ; among whom
are Robert Underwood Johnson, former Ambassador to Italy, Dr.
John H. Finlay of the New York Times, Col. Theodore Roose
velt, Otto Kahn, Richard Washburn Child, Paul D. Cravath,
Charles H. Sabin and others. Articles have appeared in The New
York Times, The Herald-Tribune, the New York American, The
Evening Sun, The Mail-Telegram, the Washington Post, The
Philadelphia Public Ledger, The Providence Journal, the Boston
Transcript, and in many other out-of-town papers.
In an editorial expressing its enthusiastic endorsement of
the campaign, the New York World declared that "it would win
the support of Americans whether or not of Italian race, who de
sire to see Latin culture and civilization better understood in the
New World, its historic importance in the development of Amer
ican thought and action more generally studied. For such study,
the Italian House in New York should serve as an inspirational
source of information.
Thanks to the very kind generosity of Mrs. Lionello Perera,
Mrs. Joseph Di Giorgio, Mrs. Gino Speranza, Comm. Almerindo
Portfolio, Loggia Garibaldi, each of whom have subscribed One
Thousand Dollars ($1,000.00), and of Comm. Beniamino Gigli of
the Metropolitan Opera House, Chev. Giovanni Girardon, Dr. De
Marinis and Dr. Paolo de Vecchi, Mr. Adamo Cirrarone, each of
whom subscribed Five Hundred Dollars ($500.00), and of many
other worthy contributors, over $20,000.00 has already been
pledged, and the prospects for establishing a permanent bureau
for the promotion of Italian culture in America were never
brighter. '
The Casa Italiana when established at Columbia University
will be available for all activities which tend to improve the in
tellectual relations between Italy and the United Stages.
It will stimulate greater interest for things Italian in Amer
ica, and for things American in Italy. It will contain a reference
and circulating library of Italian literature, science and com
merce, available for readers in all parts of the country ; it will en
courage the publication of books, essays, etc., in Italian or Eng
lish relating to Italy; it will have lecture halls and meeting
rooms; it will provide suitable receptions for distinguished Ita
lian visitors and will cooperate with other institutions in arrang
ing lectures for them. The Institute will cooperate with Colum
bia University in encouraging the home study of Italian among
Americans who are unable to attend a university or college, and
conduct correspondence courses in that language and literature.
The Institute will extend its field of operations in cooperation
with the Institute of Arts and Sciences in spreading Italian cul
ture by means of far-reaching lecture tours and radio broadcast
ing, by holding Dante Day Celebrations and awarding medals
to students for excellence in the study of Italian.
In short the Italian House, in connection jvith the Institute
of Italian Culture will be the central bureau of information and
activity concerning Italo-American intellectual relations, and as
a result, it will not only help materiali- to strengthen the bonds
of friendship between the two countries, but will contribute to
give to Americans a more enlightened and sympathetic view of
the economic, political and cultural problems of the Italian peo
ple and vice-versa.
The National Executive Committee proposes to raise an
initial fund of Two Hundred and Fifty Thousand Dollars to pur
chase and equip the Italian House ami endow the Tnstitute with
a fund with which to carry on its future work. y
If you are a true lover of Italy and have the best interests of
the Italian people at heart, send your contribution, no matter
how small, to Chev. G. Girardon, Treasurer, at No. 3!)',) Broad
way, New York City.
