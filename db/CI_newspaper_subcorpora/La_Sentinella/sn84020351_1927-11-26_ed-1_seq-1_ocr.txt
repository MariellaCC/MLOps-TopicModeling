FIGHT LOOMS ON IMMIGRATION BAN
Renewal of the bitter fight waged last winterover the pro
posal to apply quota plan restrictions on immigration from Canada
and the Latin-American contries is one of the certainties of the
coming session of congress.
Representative Box (democrat, Texas) who first suggest ex
tension of the quota system to- countries of the western hemisp
here in 1925, has announced that he will re-introduce his bill in the
coming session, and Harry E. Hull, commisionerrgeneral of immi-,
gration, is preparing to recommend, legislation embracing many
features of the Box proposal.
Trade unions, farmer's organizations, women's organizations,
social service agencies and citizen's associations particularly in
the southwest and middle west, where the effects of the Mexican
influx have been most noticeable asserting that everincreasing
immigration from Latin-America is defeating the pnrpose of the
restrictive immigration laws, are vigorously supporting the move
to raise the barriers. ' ,
Equally active in opposition to any further restriction are the
railroads which depend largely on Mexican labor for their construc
tion and maintenance work, the lumber and mining industries and
the operators of large ranches and farms in Texas, California,
New Mexico, Arizona and Colorado.
The opponents of resriction minimize the social problems
created by the Mexican and contend that their labor is indispens
able to agriculture and industry in the southwest. Limitation of
Mexican entry would "create stagnation and be of incalculable
injury to the industries of the west", according to a resolution
adopted by the National Wool Grower's Association in 1925.
And drastic restriction on immigration from Canada and the
enactment of legislation practically prohibiting immigration from
Mexico, Central and South America, would greatly jenchance the
temptation for alièns to enter the United States illegally. To pre
vent their entry absolutely would require constant watch of thou
sands of miles of northern and southern boundary a physical
impossibility, according to opponents to the plan, unless the per
sonnel of the immigration service were very largely increased and
expenditures correspondingly enlarged.
