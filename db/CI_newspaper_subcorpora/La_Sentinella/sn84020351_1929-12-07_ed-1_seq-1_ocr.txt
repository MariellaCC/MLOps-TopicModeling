DECEMBER
Winter has come. The piercing wind shrilly whistles this
fact into our ears, and the nippy air corroborates it with persist
ent zest. The ice King has arrived and is holding court with frosty
dignity. Snow mantles the earth with an ermine robe more splendid
chan that of an Emperor. "He giveth his snow like wool". Pity the
poor Hottentot who never saw a snow flake and has missed the
boisterous fun of snowballing ah unsuspecting pedestrian. And
have a care to dislike snow is a sure sign of old age.
December is . the month of the shortest days and longest
nights. We go to work in the cold grey dawn and return in the
deepening twilight. Aroused by the fiendish alarm clock at 6:30
a. m., we snuggle beneath the warm covers, postponing" the fateful
getting up time as long as possible, , while we listen to the wheels
oi the milk wagon play "From Greenland's Icy Mountains" on the
jrunching show. The lenghtened nights are a boon and a blessing,
idmitting as they do oft '
"Fireside enjoyments, home-born happiness,
And all the comforts that the lowly roof
Of undisturbed retirement, and the hours
Of long, uninterrupted evening, know."
Being the twelfth and the last of the months, December is a
favorite figure to describe the infirmities and weakness of old
age. "December and May" is a popular phrase for contrasting the
failing vision and vigor of lengthening years with buoyancy and
beauty of youth. The figure is faulty, since the closing month of
the year is the most spirited of the twelve and crammed full of
cheer and joy. "Any color so it is red," and red is typical Of
December, the red of holly, or winter berries and flaming poin
settia. The crowing glory of the month, however, is none of these
things, but the Christmas festival, with its coronation of" child-,
hood, its tender meaning and the unique opportunity to know
that "it is more blessed to give than to receive." - .

There's nothing new in the advise to "Mail Your Christmas
Presents Early" this year, but it is just as sound as ever.
Human beings, it seems, like to procrastinate. With most of
us there is a prevailing tendency to let matters wait untiL the last
moment and then go after them like it was a matter of life .and
death. - y
And the mailing of Christmas presents is no exception

THE CHRISTMAS SEAL
Within a short time the annual sale of Christmas seals by the
Jational Tubercolosis Association will begin ; and now is a good
'me to remind ourselves to buy as many as we possibly can
manage. . . :
The seals sell for a penny each; yet they provide the sole
support of the association's 1400 affiliated organizations in all
parts of the United States in their fight against tubercolosis.
There is not any reason why we should make an extended
plea for the support of this. work. The prevalence of tubercolosis
s such a major problem, and the work done by these organizations
s so valuable to the entire country, that the seals ought to sell
themselves.
HE KILLED HIS MOTHER
Richard Corbett, Frechman killed his mother to relieye her
suffering from an incurable disease, was tried for murder, found
not guilty by jury. This brings to the minds of thinking men and
women once more the thought of relieving human beings from
tneir suffering even as we practice it with animals. Should a
person; tired of life, suffering untold agonies and with no hope
of recovery be allowed to suffer on and on? Of course we proceed
on the theory that no human has a right to end the life of another .
human, and when he does so it is murder, but the world would
l!old much less suffering for humanity if we treated the human
incurables as we treat the incurable domestic animals. .
THOSE WHO LIVE IN GLASS HOUSES
This "hewing to the line" and "never mind the chips" sounds
very well, but it is hard to carry out in running a community
newspaper as we have discovered in the last few weeks. While
the public has its right with respect to news, yet we have not the
heart to print stories which will do unnecessary harm to boys and
girls, still in the formative period, or stories which an inflamed
public opinion will be inclined to interpret recklessly.
Of course, there will be those who ridicule our policy and who
believe that sensation-should be the policy. But some of these
same folks are apt to walk into the office before long and say,
"Please don't say anything about this" and they will be very
grateful for our thoughtf ulness.
Which is only another way of saying that most of us either
have glass houses or plenty of windows. .
