CHRISTOPHER COLUMBUS
Between the middle Jges and the modem era stands a mighty
figure, Columbus, reflecting radiantly the glories and the strug
gles of the days gone before him and foreshadowing the promise
and the achievements of the times that followed. Clearly he
typifies both periods in history. A chivalrous spirit, ever inclined
toward courtesy and genality, soft-spoken though animated by
intense pride, blind to fear, the embodiment of patience and
loyalty, at heart a Crusader for his Faith, prejudiced, by the con
victions of his day and steeped in its limitations. Yet, a trail
blazer, an investigator of the highest and most scientific t pe who
puts his observations above the accepted traditions of the hour,
driving ever onward in his quest, trying tp realize the idea that had
determined his conduct. What he observed, he drew conclusions
from, and these conclusions he believed in, and he made the
world believe him, too. We have in Columbus the mari of faith
and the man of experiment. At that time, he was both of the past
and of the future.
And so stands gloriously forth today, the first inter-world
figure. Spain claims him, Italy claims him, so does Portugal and
America. To none of these he belongs. He belongs to the world,
the whole of mankind.
His birth, his boyhood and death are shadowed in mystery.
Some historians have sought to detract from the wonder of his
achievement, some have ranked him with the adventurous travel
ers of his tim ctonie have sought to refer the glory of his work to
Scandinavian seafarers, some would have him in .all things greater
than the day in which he lived, and some accept his discovery as
a probability which might have fallen to the lot of some other
fortunate explorer. Space forbids an answer here. But briefly,
it maye be said that the fate of all great men has not nee n spared
to him.'. Litde uen will whisper their criticisms ?r-they have
always done, race prejudices will urge their writings, and the
relish, that mean men take in detracting from achievement, has
caused some men to become guilty of the folly which has be
trayed theirmeanness for them.
Hear Columbus :
"High was that mission to me unworthy given.
But hardship trained my hands. Firm hope made whole
My weakness lending to my spirit wings
. Across the deep to fly. When hope grew frail
Sad poverty came and with her slow calm smile
Gave me the kiss of Peace, and made me strong,
So dowered with patience, hope, faith, charity,
A beggar at the gates of that new world
I stood whose key I held, and I alone!"
NEWSPAPER EDITORS ARE LOYAL CITIZENS OF
THEIR TOWNS
If all the people in a town were as loyal to the town as the
editor of the old home newspaper, what a wonderful difference it
would make.
Mail order houses would be known only in history. The
editor is alwa3's up and doing, never ceasing, even if at times he
gets little or no encouragement. He leaves unsaid that which
might cast reflection on the town, and emphasizes the good
things.
He is a -booster for better schools and better churches, civic
pride and highest citizenship. He is forever telling of the live
business men ; that they carry a larger, better class of goods and
sell them cheaper than any other place on earth. He favors good
roads so that the people may get to town to patronize the home
merchant.
He is in favor of public improvements of whatever nature
that will help to make the old home town a more desirable place
in which to live. He is the most liberal donor in the town.
Is there any person more beneficial to a town than the news
paper man? If there is. name him.
HOW THE SMALL CAN DO IT.
There is little wonder that the city merchants attract cus
tomers from both city and outlying districts and other counties.
They realize the virtue of advertising, and they set aside certain
amounts to promote new business and hold what they have.
Take for instance the New Haven papers, they are filled with
attractive advertising, and the people read them, and go to the
places that advertise their merchandise. The small town mer
chant must in self-defense advertise his merchandise or allow his
customers to graduali- drift to the cities to make their purchases.
In proportion to population, the city papers have no larger circu
lation than the weekly papers that would'reach the buying public
in the counties that they circulate in. If the small town merchant
expects to hold' his own, with good roads, bus lines and auto
mobiles, he must change his method and advertise and reach the
public with what he has to offer.
