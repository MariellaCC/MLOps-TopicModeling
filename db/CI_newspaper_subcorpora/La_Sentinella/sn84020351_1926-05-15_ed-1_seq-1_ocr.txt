THE PARKING PROBLEM.
The parking problem finally grows acute in all towns of any
size until something is done about it. It is an ever-present per
plexity which the city fathers are forced to grapple with, and if
possible to solve with least inconvenience to the community.
Pretty nearly every town has parking regulations that shoW
variations from every other town.
Bridgeport has practically no parking laws and throws the
streets open, even where business traffic is most congested to
whomever gets there first. There is no time limit on the use of
any particular parking space, so the streets in reality are con
verted into piivate parking spaces for those who preempt them
to the exclusion of everybody else. As the streets belong equally
to all, it is conferring a considerable monopoly on a few certain
favored ones where they arc permitted to have all the parking
rights, simply because they are first to arrive. It is a vexed is
sue, and one not readily handled. But it is universally agreed
that not to have any sort of surveillance over those who push
their cars into the curb anywhere, there to remain for indefinite
periods, is unfair to the rest

PLANTING THE GARDEN.
It seems rather hard and dirty work to folks who like to
keep their white cuffs clean. But to the educated gardener it is"
an adventurous sport. It seems so incomprehensible, as he
sticks those puny little seeds in the ground, seemingly no more
important than little tiny peKljles, thaf they should ever blossom
forth in sweet flowers and juicy vegetables.
A few days later, when they stick their little noses above
the ground, and start on their summer work, the wonder of the
transformation never ceases to impress him. It makes him feel
as if he were a partner of great unseen forces that make the
world go around.
The experienced gardner finds pleasure in using his skill so
as to favor the little seed things, so as to give them their best
start in the world, and he somehow feels that they are thanking
him for making the obstacles in the way of growth as slight as
possible. . t "
WHAT'S THE USE?
What's the use of scrapping and quarreling every time you
fail to have your own way about things? What is accomplished
by wrangling over unimportant details, anyway? Sometimes you
get what you want, more often you do not, but in either case the
result is affected but little through wrangling and fussing. The
person who remains calm under annoying circumstances has a
better mind than the one inclined to "fly off the handle" at a
momen't notice. The fellow who spits on his hands, cracks his
heels together and threatens to clean up all who disagree with
him, only advertises to the world that he is rattlebrained and
equipped with but one resource, a show of force. Even many
of the things that seem of tremendous importance at the moment,
fade or entirely disappear with time. Not so with the mean
things said and done in spite and anger. The petty selfishness
that is exposed, the littleness and the intolerance exhibited in dis
putes and wrangles, live long in the memories of people who
might otherwise be your friends. If this be preaching then make
flic most of it.

not put off by the doorman or the secretary. The big men are
