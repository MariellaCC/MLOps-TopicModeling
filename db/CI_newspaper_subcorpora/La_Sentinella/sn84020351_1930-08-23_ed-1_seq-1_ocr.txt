GARDENS OF ITALY x '
By Mary McKeefe
How many of the people who yearly flock to her shores know
that Italy is the land of beautiful gardens? Her people are innately
lovers of the beautiful and, adapting the natural loveliness of their
land, they have made it one of the most picturesque in the world.
From the stateliest vistas of the Villa d'Este, on the outskirts xf
Tivoli, to the loweliest hovel we find gardens, surpassed by none.
Because to those who know and love Italy's gardens, the Villa
d'Este, which has recently been restored by the Government, is
most familiar, let us journey there first. Leaving Rome over the
highway by which, it is said, Hadrian was wont to travel we pass
his country estate. . .
Winding through the narrow streets at Tivoli we reach the
villa. Here, after descending through the crumbling ruins of the
house itself, we stand on the highest terrace of the garden. Before
us a wide stairway leads to the formal gardens below. Down ita
balustrades are cascade miniature waterfalls and buiet pools at the
bottom. About are fountains of every description.
As her ancient ruins are synonymous with Rome so fountains
are synonymous with Italian gardens. They buble forth every
where, from under a rocky crevice, from the center of a deep pool.
They have been usd in a unique manner in this garden where rock
formations have been moulded to resemble " Rome. Laid out in
exquisite detail, we identify the Forum, the Coliseum, the Seven
Hills, and from these heights we look out toward the Sabine hills,
across the valley where Cicero, in his later years, had his farm.
At first landing of the stairway is an endless vista of fount
ains. They throw their silvery sprays to the heavens through
arches of ageles cypress trees. The path beside them leads to a
giant waterfall, thundering down from a heisrht of some 100 feet
and veiling a cloistered walk beneath in a cloud ofjnist.
The cloister leads to an organ loft directly behind the cascade
where an organ played by the wind and the water sings a veritable
song of nature.
As the gardens of d'Este are noted for their fountains so the
gardens of the Villa Gueste at Verona are renowned for their
ivygrown Cypréjss trees. Laid out on the side of a sheer cliff, this
garden rambles over the aged roots gaining a foothold where it
would . seem impossible. It is beyond the memory of many men
when these hoary trees were young.
Their trunks are gnarled and bent as they cling to the side
of the cliff but their beauty is a timeless beauty and the scars of
centuries are mercifully hidden beneath a blanker of luxuriant
iyy. Here too, are fountains, but the are picturesque in a quaint
rather than a splendid way. We come upon the spring form the
side of a hill or hidden beneath the giant roots of the trees.
. At Villa Serbelloni, high above Bellagio, on Lake Como,' there
are old" palm ' tréétó. "Situated on a promontory which commands
a view on three sides of the lake, its vistas of sky and water linger
long in memory.
Italy is indeed a land of gardens. If one tires of contemplating
her splendid monuments of the past, her astounding progress
toward the future, they are on every hand to rest the eye and
comfort the spirit.
