CITES OUR INDUSTRY AS IDEAL FOR ITALY
American industrial organization was held up as an ideal
toward, which Italian industrialist should aim, by the Minister of
National Economy, Signor Belluuzzo.
"I. wish to speak of American industrial organization," he
said, "not because I believe that conditions in the Ignited States .
and Italy are in any way comparable or. because I believe that the
rhythm of production and wages existing in America can be
reached here, but merely to indicate what can be done in the way
of industrial organization and to point out how much ground we
in Italy still have to travel." .
Signor Belluzzo attempted to answer the question, "Why is it
that the American industry can compete with our own, though ;
paying its workmen a daily wage which, in some cases, exceéds
what we pay them in a week" - . !
In the first place, he said that, the United States has such a :
vast internal market that their industries can specialize and
organize technically in such a way as to get more work out of each
worker.. ' " , : , -" ' :
oecondly , that American wTorkmen work with - a discipline,
intensity and speed that is not known in Italy.
Thirdly that American organization is such that each worker -must
produce to the utmost capacity, otherwise Tie is automatically -eliminated.
Many believe, added Signor Belluzzo, that the applica
tion of American methods in Italy , would be harmfull because, by
increasing the output of each single worker, it w ould leàd to wide
spread unemployment: This, he stated, is a fallacy.
Finally, Signor Belluzzo urged Italian producers to proceed
along the path which has been blazed by America. 1 . : -
"Italian industrialist", he said, "must have the courage to r
look the situation in the face. We must suppress industries which '
cannot be organized in a. modern fashion and we must unite our

PULLING THE WEEDS, ,
About now comes the toughest end of the gardening business,
the eradication of those weeds that threaten the life of your
plants. ; Even though your vegetables and flowers need careful
tending and fertilization, the" weeds flourish in the driest soil.
With malignant persistency, they rear their dirty I heads and grow
with direful vigor, eating out the cream of the plant food.
Only merciless warfare can keep them down. It is hot, dusty
and dirty work for the gardeners of Bridgeport. But the garden
feels much happier after you have rescued it from these intruders.
