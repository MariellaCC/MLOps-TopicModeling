VANDALS ON THE MARCH. .
Just as usual, the opening season has mobilized its army of
vandals and sent them forth into forest, prairie, and mountain
reaches to pilfer, litter, and destroy. Grass plots will 'soon be
marred by accumulated waste where careless tourists have
paused for an hour and gone on their careless way. Blossom
laden branches will be torn from their mother trees, wild flowers
will be picked over and trampled underfoot, and the beauty of
the countryside will be pock-marked by grasping hands. The
wanton disregard of many tourists will render the season almost
unendurable to those whose lot is cast in the rural districts.
Not for long would blase unbanities endure an invasion of
rustics on mischief bent and leaving the footprints of vandalism
on much-prized city lawns. The police power would be invoked
in a moment should ruralites invade the private property of city
dwellers and. appropriate flowers and shrubs at will. The coun
try dwellei lacks this police protection and too often finds him
self helpless when discourteous tourists invade his premises and
lay rude hands on everything in reach. But police protection
should never be needed to protect the flowers and shrubbery of
the farm. Those who drive out into the open spaces should be
sufficiently courteous to render unto others the same treatment
they would ask for themselves.
UP TO MOTORISTS
American railroads are planning another summer drive
against the reckless driver at grade crossings. The campaign will
run from June 1 until September 1, a period of the year marked
by the reddest trail of blood.
No person seriously disputes that the vast majority of grade
crossing accidents are the result of carelessness on the part of
the victims. The practice is obvious to any observer at these
points. Since all crossings cannot be eliminated at once, caution
alone must be taught. And that is difficult in a rush-a-day world,
but it is a lesson for every motorist to master.

STUDENT MARRIAGES.
Colleges and universities are adopting a rule that students
who marry during the school year will be dropped. The Chicago
Tribune remarks : "It's a very reasonable rule. A college educa
tion is a costly business. 'To most parents it means sacrifice to
send their children away for four years of higher education. They
send them to acquire knowledge and not to get married. The
student who is thinking of marriage is not thinking very much
about studies. It is a pleasant but thoughtless way of defeating
the purposes for which the parents made a sacrifice. There is
time enough for marriage after graduation."
