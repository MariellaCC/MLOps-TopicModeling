NON-VOTING CITIZENS
It is reported that Philadelphia alone contains half million
citizens who are eligible to vote, but whose names are not on the
assessment lists. They pay nò taxes, do no voting, enjoy the pro
tection of our laws but shirk the duties of citizenship.
, We have heard it said that Fairfield County contains a very
considerable number of such citizens. We have often been told
that our district contains a goodly number of persons who are not
i egistered and who are never asked to pay taxes. Some even boast
that they pay nothing to the support of the local or county
government.
The quality of such citizenship leaves very much to be desired.
Men and women who enjoy the protection of just laws should be
sufficiently conscious of their obligations to joyously seek the
opportunity of paying a little annually to the support of the goven
ment. They, should likewise be interested in the promotion of
good government in city, county and state.
FARM
Both of the prominent political parties, it seems to us, are
overlooking a very simple solution on the Farm Relief Program.
If, due to a combination of hard work and favorable weather,
the farmer is enabled to grow bumper crops this year for instance,
crops that exceed domestic or foreign demand, competition will
naturally lower the prices to be obtained.
If, for example, he grows two bushels of potatoes for which
in a big crop year he received only 50 cents a bushel, contrasted
with a lean crop year when a bushel of these tubers yelds 90 cents
bushel, Mr. Hoover or Mr. Smith could pull many farmer votes
his way by a promise that a law would be enacted compelling the
consuming public to eat two potatoes in big crop years to one
potato in lean crop years.
Should, however, the political adviser think that this plan
wuold lose too many consumer votes, the candidates could then
promise the farmer that the government would take and pay for
all farm products grown, in excess of a certain limited amount
which would maintain prices on high level. Somebody might fool
ishly raise the question as to what disposition the government
would make of its purchase. The government would of course
destroy its purchase and add the amount paid to the tax roll.
If either of the two above suggested plans worked out favo
rably in solving the farm problem, then they could be applied to all
manufactured articles. We play no favorites, hence either the
Democrats or Republicans are at liberty to avail themselves of
our suggestions. , 1 .

There is probably nothing more dangerous on a highway at
night than an automobile with only one light ; and invariably the -light
is on the right side of the car. Of course the law requires
that two lights be displayed on the front of every automobile, but
it is impossible to enforce such a law without officers. There are
always those willing to take a chance driving machines with only
one light, and few of these ever' stop .to consider that they are
endangering not only their own lives but the livens of others in
automobiles approaching from the direction in which they are
headed.
The road patrol men would not be sent out to "nab" speeders,
for it is realized that the speedeng automobile, when properly
handled, often is the least dangerous type on the roads. But those
who drive with only one light, pass other cars on curves and when
approaching the high points of grades and generally fail to conduct
whemselyes in such a manner that others are not placed in jeopardy
vvould be prey for the patrolmen. We believe that establishment
of such a force would go a long way toward cutting down the
death list from automobile accidents a list which is assuming
tremendous proportions in Connecticut.
THE ELECTRIC NATION
It is undoubtedly a fact that the people of this nation do not
realize the tremendous advantages they are enjoying over the
people of other nations, as a result of the development which has
aken place in our electric industry during the past 10 years.
Electricity is a necessity in even our smallest homes because
;t is a labor saver and the cheapest and most efficient form of
illumination.
The development and interconnection of large central station
power plants has given 24 hour electric service to the majority of
ihe smallest communities in our country. Residents in these small
centers of population receive service which people living in many
)i the largest nations in the world, do not enjoy. Further, they
. eceive such service at rates which, as a general rule, are unknown
in countries other than ours.
The people of the United States use nearly as much electricity
as all the rest of the world combined. Behind this remarkable
service which is today being extended from the cities to the farms,
lies the driving force of private initiative and enterprise which is
responsible for the electrical development we enjoy. Millions of
investors have poured their savings into securities of our electric
companies and it is said that this industry is today absorbing
inore new capital to furnish even greater service, than is being
absorbed by any other Hne of industrial activity.
