KEEP TAXES DOWN
The best advice that can be given to communities anxious to
expand and develop industrially is keep taxes down.
High tax rates and industrial development do not go together.
The community that is too fast in providing "improvements" by
excessive taxation is foredoomed to restricted growth.
Industry today is going in a new and interesting direction
toward decentralization. Where once, only centrally located cities
had an opportunity to become industrially great, the boundry
town has an important share in the new movement.
There are small towns today that tomorrow will be out
standing industrial centers. And chief among the advantages
they must offer will be a low tax rate.
There are other towns which can offer many expensive
attractions. Yet they will be passed over by industry, because
they have made the mistakes of spending money wastefully with
resulting high tax rates.
Reasonable taxes invite progress, development and prosperity.
High taxes discourage the investor and builder.
THE PIONEER SPIRIT
Who says the pioneer spirit is dead ? Listen to this :
Two young men, in New York, became involved in argument,
the nub of which was that alcohol is not the only stimulant that
will produce the "desired effect". One contended that milk, gas
oline and ether would serve as well as corn whisky or canned
heat. To prove his point, he mixed such a drink and poured it
down. The other, not to be outdone, did likewise.
As these two mistaken youths recover in the hospital, they
can thank their lucky stars that the mixture "got" them before
they had time to take a second drink. Otherwise the story, might
have had a more tragic ending; and the brave pioneers' spirits
would not now be pondering the folly of embrarking on such
daring and courageous enterprises, with nothing at all as the goal.

AN OPINON FROM CANADA
In a searching report that is very fair to both sides of the
controversy the Canadian Automobile Underwriters Association
has reached the conclusion that compulsory automobile liability
insurance would be a failure from both economic and accident
prevention standpoints.
It would not reduce accidents and it would very possibly raise
the cost of insurance. In a number of types of accidents that are
responsible for a large proportion of injuries it would not provide
indemnity. And in all cases where the injured, party is held
responsible he would not be identified.
It would increase the number of claims pressed and increase
litigation. And it might increase careless driving, instead of de
creasing it as proponents of the law have claimed.
Tn the United States our experience with compulsory insur
ance has been discouraging. Massachusetts, the first of our states
to experiment with compulsory automobile insurance, has ex
perienced a new effect that of fake claims, made to the compa
re es and the courts. The obvious result of a law that provides
many loopholes must be to raise insurance costs without solving
the problem at which the law is aimed, namely, accident prevent
ion and compensation when due.
