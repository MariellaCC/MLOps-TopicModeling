MUNICIPAL OWNERSHIP LIMITS SERVICE
Jackson, Mississippi, illustrates why municipal ownership of
vital public utilities is not al ways, practical. Jackson refuses to
extend municipal water mains beyond the corporate limits, regard
less of how many consumers may be offered as an inducement.
Since the flotation of the late bond issue, the city, has been
besieged with petitions to extend water mains beyond corporate
limits, even to remote territories. To comply would possibly have
cost twice as much as the bond issue.
The Mayor said: "If light and gas plants desire to. extend
service beyond the city limits, we have no quarrel with them, but
it is neither reasonable nor just to ask us to carry water and
sewerage lines beyond the borders of the corporations".
And that tells the whole story. A private plant can build
extensions wherever the business justifies. A municipality which
is not pr imarily a business enterprise but a governing and a police
organization, when it goes into business sells bonds" which become
a lien against every property owner within its borders. It is obvio
usly unfair to obligate property within corporate limits to build
extensions outside of these limits. For this reasons, municipal
plants are always local institptions, dependent entirely on local
conditions and subject to complete destruction by local calamities. x
Electric, gas and telephone service could never be maintained
as we enjoy them, if dependance was placed in little local plants.
Interconnected power lines with resulting benefits to all would be
impossible. -
If no other reason, and there are many, this great drawback
should cause any community to hesitate before tying up one of
vital public utilities with the inelastic and limiting bands of
municipal ownership.
