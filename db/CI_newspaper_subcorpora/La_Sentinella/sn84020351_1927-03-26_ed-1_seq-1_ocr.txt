THE POSTAL PROBLEM
The fact that the Goverment, unintentionally, perhaps, has
concealed the moderate prosperity of the commercial branches of
the postal service behind a traditional deficit caused by an ove
load of free or less-than-cost "policy" services, is disclosed in a
report -which the postal-service committee of Chamber of Com
merce of the United States has submitted to Congress as a basis
for demanding revision of the postal rates.
The Post Office Department is the errand boy for all Govern
ment departments, agencies and commissions. It carries free all
the pamplets sent out by the Department of Agriculture, the
notices sent out by the Treasury Department, the voluminous
mail sent out by every bureau, commission, committee and depar
tment comprising, in the aggregate, Goverment business. Yet the
Post Office Department pays the Bureau of Engraving for its
stamps, it pays the Goverment Printing Office for its printing
and it pays every other department for what it gets.
The cost of performing "policy services" that is, services
recognized to be in the public interest but uneconomic from the
pureiy commercial viewpoint, such as rural free delivery and the
handling of fraternal and scientific jmblications are charged
against the Post Office Department, n v v ; ;r. .
In consequence, the ghost of the deficit hovers about thè Post
Office Department and the perennial efforts to lay raising postal
rates, placing additional burbens upon the commercial mail users,
have prove ineffective. . , ' "
Such practice is unfair t the Post Office Department as well
as to private users of the. mails, and would not be tolerated , by
any well regulated, private business concern.
Raising, postal rates has an effect on business just opposite,
to that of reducing taxes. The latter has been stimulating and
the former, as now shown by experience, has been regarding. - '
Information received from many lines of industry confirms
the conclusion that the present postal rates, most of which went
into effects upon business and harmful to the general public wel
fare but also in certain respects fail to accomplish the purpose
for which they were intended.
For istance, the increase in the rate on private mailing cards
from one to two cents not only failed to produce more revenue,
but drove more than 80 per cent of private post cards out business.
All service performed by the . Post Office Department for
other divisions of the 4 Government should be charged to the
departments receiving it. , . ; f
When this has been accomplished the postal laws and mailing
rates should be revised accordingly, for they require a thorough
overhauling.
