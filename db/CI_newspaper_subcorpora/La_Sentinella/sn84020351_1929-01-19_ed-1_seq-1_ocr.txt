THE ONE TRACK
MIND
By Dr. Joseph Pantaleone
I believe it was the great Woodrow Wilson who originally
coined the phrase "one) track-niind". He meant to apply it to
that type of specialist who became so narrowed in his view of
life that he could very well be considered a monstrosity. Such
men are in my opinion lop-sided caricatures. There is a tendency
in modern life to over-specialize and while this is in itself not only
essential and fundamental to progress still there is danger of
losing sight of our relation to the rest of life and of becoming
decidely narrow-minded and thus in the end of doing more harm
than good.
As I see it, life can be roughly compared to an extremely
complicated and very extensive machine. To understand any of
its parts thoroughly we must consider their relation to the rest
of the madrine in its entirety. In other words we must look at the
work we are doing in relation to the rest of the entire universe.
If we really wish to understand and to solve the problems ahead
of us in any line of business or other activity, it is always best to
consider the relation of our "problems to a ll the rest of the human
affairs. We shall in this way obtain a true picture of their actual
nature and we shall thus be less liable to make mistakes than we
would have been, and we simply looked at the problem as some
thing isolated and distinct and entirely apart from the rest of the
universe. The one track-mind insists on knowing all the intimate
details and irregularities concerning one little cog-wheel in a very
complex machine, but it is emphatically inclined to refuse to know
anything else about the rest of the machine. What is the result?
Simply this: by his blindness this individual has missed the
significance and the purpose of his cog-wheel; he has missed the
reason for its position, shape and size and he is thus entirely
ignorant of its real meaning and may even conceive it in an
entirely false way. As a consequence his opinion on the subject
may not only be erroneous but it may even be dangerous. You
will say such a man is mentally unbalanced and I agree with you.
But so are those specialists in any field of human endeavor who
refuse to consider the rest of life and confine all their attentions
to the cog-wheel of their activity. What chance have these blind
men of making any fundamental contribution to the progress of
their specialty? Very little, if any. They may discover more
details, but they will be meaningless to them until some great
co-ordinating mind come along and by the simple process of
considering the relation of the parts to whole shows them the
significance of phenomena which at first seemed destined to
remain eternal mysteries.
And this has been widely illustrated in the history of all
human progress. Who have been the great leaders in any field?
They have been men who have taken countless factors, into
consideration. These men early realized that all things in life
were inter-related and that to understand the nature of any one
problem thoroughly, we should delve into all iyngleA. and .look

the universe. But to be able to do this is not by any means an
easy matter, for it takes an intellectual giant to see all the complex

field of action. And also we find that in science, in art, in reform,

creative geniuses who have contributed most to the progress of
their particular field, have been men of great versatility and
varied activity. All things interested them and they looked at
their problem always with reference to the rest of the universe.
While not every one can be a master mind and have the
opportunities of these chosen few, still all of us can look at our
activities in. a broader more balanced and saner way. For example:
we can all consider the effect of our action on" those about us and
on humanity as a whole ; whether we are harming or contributing
to the welfare of our fellow beings. We can all start analyzing
our lives in detail noticing the marvelous relations existing
between all things and we can all try hard and sincerely to do our
best in arriving at our conclusions and opinions, by taking as many
factors as possible into consideration. If we are lawyers let us
realize that ministers and doctors are also parts of our great
civilization and that law is not everthing. Let also realize the
relation of law to psychology, to history, to economics, to science,
to religion, to philosophy, ect. If we are teachers let us realize
the value of knowing psychology, history, philosophy, science, art,
reform, poetry, sociology, ect. And in this connection I may say
that I am convinced that if our ministers and priests had only had
an opportunity to delve deeply into science, our religion today
would be on a much higher spiritual plane than it actually is now.
The most successful man in any profession or business will
be found to be the man who knows a great deal about many other
things besides his narrow chosen field. He is very enthusiastic
and deeply interested in all human activity. This man will turn
out to be of more value to humanity than all the one track-minds
who have lost all right senses of proportion, lost all sight of
perspective.
Let me give you some historical examples of men who have
been leaders in their line and whose versatility was a prominent
characteristic of their genius. Virchow, the founder of cellular
pathology, was also a great statesman and educator and a man
of great power in Berlin, when he was in his prime. Joseph
Priestley, the great chemist, the discover of oxygen was a great
religious teacher and author before he; became a scientist. Ben
jamin Franklin was a scientist, a writer, a diplomat, a philosopher,
an inventor and a great organizer. Professor Albert Einstein, the
founder of relativity, is a great violinist, a poet, astronomer,
physicist, mathematician and philosopher. Leonardo da Vinci the
great engineer, architect, painter, scultor, musician, poet, astron
omer, physician, philosopher, mathematician, ect. was probably
the greatest versatile genius the world has ever seen. Just to
mention a few of countless others I shall limit myself to naming,
Goethe, Wagner, Shakespeare, Plato, Aristotle, Christ, Solomon,
Moses and Dante, all of them men of the widest culture and human
interest whose knowledge of life as a whole was astonishing in
deed. The lesson we should learn from these mighty men is to
cultivate an interest in life as a whole for then we shall enjoy it
all the more and our work shall be of greater service to our fellow
beings. Francis Bacon said ; Bene vixit qui bene latuit. This simply
means that he who lives a real life, must indulge in a little of
everything. This does not mean that we are to be "jacks-of -all-trades"
for this is also an unbalanced attitude toward life. But
we should try to strike a happy medium and while we should
avoid the pitfalls of the one track-mind we should still beware of
the evils of a "jack-of-alltrade" attitude, and herein is seen the
practical values of attaining a golden mean. It is a difficult
problem and each one must solve it for himself.
But now let us look at the question from the scientist's point
of view. In modern science there is a great need of the specialist.
But I must say that I am convinced that a great many scientists
today have one-track minds. As a consequence of this attitude
science suffers and the progress of the race is retarded. We need
the specialist, but he must be a man of understanding and breadth
of view, not a one track-mind who does ultimately more harm
than good.
