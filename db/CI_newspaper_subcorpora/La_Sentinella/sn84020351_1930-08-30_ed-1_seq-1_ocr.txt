CONCENTRATES ON NEWSPAPERS
After a thorough and extended investigation of the various
plans for advertising, the Fox Film Company, a concern that
operates hundreds of picture theatres in the United States, has
given instructions to its house managers to concentrate all their
advertising appropriations on space in newspapers. They are not
to do this at the sacrifice of any appropriation decide upon when
various schemes of advertising were decided upon, but to increase
space in the newspaper. The reasons for this departure fròpi
former methods are given by the director of advertising. The
reasons number six. and are: "Frequently the theatres must
make quick changes, in programs. These can always be advertised
in newspaper columns. The newspaper columns also permit the
best use of decorative advertising, which is so essential, to the
advertising of our theatres. Increased space is always available
in newspaper columns. Newspapers advertisements can be changed
or corrected with greater dispatch, minimum cost and greatest
conveninece. Except in rare cases, theatrical advertisements in
the newspapers do not have to compete with non-theatrical copy.
Newpaper advertisiments can be digested at the convenience of
the reader." Th decision was based largely on the experience of
the Roxy theatre in New York, which has -used newspaper
advertising esclusively. ,

. AMERICAN FARMER
It is becoming more and more difficult every day for political
parties to close their eyes to the ruinous effect of continually
falling prices on the position of the .American Farmer, while his
costs of production remain practically the same. The index
number of prices has fallen from 48 per cent above pre-war levels
in January last to 34 per cent last week, while wage costs are
from 100 to 110 per cent higher. It means that the bumper crops
of last year all over the world have been a tragedy for the
American farmer, and yet no one can pretend that the American
consumer is conscious of having reaped any material advantage.

This is the "invocation" delivered by Rev. T. D. Bateman,
of the First Presbyterian Church, Columbus, Miss.; at the annual
convention of the Mississippi Press Association, held in that city:
"Eternal God, our Heavenly Father, we ask thy blessing
upon these newspaper people who are wont to communicate with
the ends of the earth. They are people who can smell a revolution
in China, people who have a license to enter the gates of prices
and potentates, but who will have a mighty hard time getting
inside the gates of Paradise.
"Have mercy on these people who are-tempted by every devil,
from the printer's devil to the devils of newspaper headlines,
and these same who are so busy listening to , the sounds of the
wires and the songs of the women that offtimes.the voice of God
finds no listener among them and no place in their hearts.
"Have mercy, O Lord, upon the people who are chased by the
Colonial Dames, W. C. T. U., Kiwanis, and Chamber of Commerce.

Methodist, Presbyterian and Episcopalian, those who came over
on the Mayflower, those who came over in the Cauliflower.
"Have mercy, O Lord, on these people and help them in their
clash from ballroom to church and back to run-runners, and who

though it is so hard for newspaper men to get into the Kingdom,
may they receive a warmer welcome in Jerusalem than they do in
