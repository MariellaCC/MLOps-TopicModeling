"QUIT BUYING BOOTLEG LIQUOR
President Herbert Hoover, in his inaugural address on Monday
did not mince words dealing with the proibition situation in the
United States, and the cry of lack of law obedience.
In plain words President Hoover told a lot of persons who
call themselves good American citizens that their duty in the
present era and in the preservation of respect for law is "quit
buying booleg liquor".
While President Hoover plans to have the prohibition situation
thoroughly investigated at once, it is certain that he will never hit
the nail on the head any more directly than in the brief treatment
of the prohibition situation in his inaugural.
The perpetuation of the booze business in the United States
is made possible by the action of manypersons who consider
themselves good law abiding citizens, in", purchasing bootleg
liquor.
It is their duty to uphold the law to quit the purchase of
bootleg liquor, as the president maintains.

EUROPE'S STRONG
Though postwar governments have placed several checks
upon the liberty of migration, they have simultaneously striven to
better the lot of those who migrate both during the journey and
while they are living in the new land.
In pre-war days almost anyone could go everywhere he choose.
Unless he was an unusually well-informed person or had advice
from experienced relatives or friends he was at the mercy of
exploiters from the time he decided to leave home until he had got"
a good start in the promised land. Paralleling the familiar stories
ol quick rise from peanut vender or bootblack to capitalist, from
pick and shovel wielder to political power, there were countless
familiar stories of misery, illness and failure on the part of
immigrants who never escaped the clutches of the exploiters.
Social welfare organizers and governmental agencies did much
to protect and assist, the newcomer to the United States and other
leading countries of immigration, but on the whole his lot remained
a hard one, because his worst exploiters were often beyond the
reach of these friendly agencies.
Nowdays Governments manifest a keener interest in the
conditions under which their nationals are induced to go abroad,
the hygienic and moral conditions under which they travel, the
terms under which they labor abroad and safeguards offered them
in case of sickness, accident or other misfortune. Greater precau
tions are taken to see that only such emigrants as seem likely to
prove acceptable to the country of immigration are permitted to
board ship.
Thus the tragedy of the emigrant who spends, his savings
and perhaps borrow money for a long journy overseas: only to
be turned down at the port of entry occurs less frequently than
in pre-war days. Nor is there the congestion and disease-spread-,
ing filth of pre-war emigrant travel. Much of the extortion pract
iced by the old-time immigration agent has been eliminated Ty
Government supervision.

yearning except his beloved seed catalog. His business suffers,
perhaps, from neglect, and he shows up at Sunday School with no
more idea of what the lesson is about than a doodle, but he knows
how squashes and spaghetti should be planted and cultivated, for
his catalog has told him all about it.
A strong thing about "catalogitis" is the fact that thè sufferer
isn't conscious of any suffering whatever. He's just crazy as
bat out of purgatory, and doesn't know it.
