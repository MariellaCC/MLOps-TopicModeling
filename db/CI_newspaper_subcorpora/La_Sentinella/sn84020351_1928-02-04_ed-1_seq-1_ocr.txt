A LINE FENCE AND A LAWYER WITH COMMON SENSE
"He's got eight feet of my land and I want to commence suit
against him right away". It was with these neighborly remarks
that he busted in on our lawyer friend one morning several weeks
ngo.
"I think" said the lawyer, "that I will write your neighbor
and ask him to come in and see me. Perhaps there is a little mis
understanding that can be ironed out, save both of you some
money, and you can still be friends".
"Misunderstanding nothing. He's got eight feet of my land,
he knows it, and I'm going to have it back if I have to go to the
highest court and mortgage my farm."
Our legal friend told his client that was just about what
would happen to him if he had his way about it. And so, with
this as a background, we shorten the traces and tell you just how
thing turned out.
The lawyer had both parties meet in his office, and after a
iittle preliminary talk, the offender said, "You see it's like this,
Mr. Lawyer. I've always lived in town until I bought this farm.
and when I went there I found the fences in bad shape. I didn't
Know who my neighbors were, but I wanted to be on good terms
with them. And so, when I came to this particular fence which
was half down, I supposed of course it was on the line, and 1
thought I would build it through at my own expense, rather than
say anything to my neighbor about it. I'm awfully sorry this
happened, but friendly neighbors mean more to me than a few
feet of land, so I tell you what I'll do, I'll set the fence over ten
feet at my own expense." :
. The farmer who claimed the land jumped up, grabbed his
neighbor by the hand and said, "You won't do anything of the
kind ! You leave that fence right where it is and, by the way,
I've just butchered a hog, and when I get home, I'm. going to
bring you over half of it. And now let's all go out and get some
thing to eat on me."
This is a true story, all of which goes to prove that, with
fairly decent people most of life's troubles are caused by mis
understandings.

IF YOU WONT SIGN, DONT SAY IT
A great many of us have acquired the unfortunate faculty
of talking too much. We purport to know too much about the
other fellow and his personal business affairs. Without making
sure of our facts we proceed to collect and pass out gossip. A great
many persons, as soon as they see or hear of something a little
suspicious, proceed to make it the shaky foundation for their next
conversation.
One of thej-feirf - boet. methods to pursue in trying to break
ourselves of this pernicious hàìbit of peddlmgidlèòssipis'tiàDpt
and live up to the following rule:
Whenever you are tempted to tell a bit of gossip you have
ìeard, or whenever you see something that you do not under
stand, give it this test before you tell it; Would you be willing
to write out, sign your name to and pass around the gossip which
you have heard or the suspicions which have been aroused in you
by what you have seen or heard? Could you honestly say that
what you are about to say or repeat would be right, and that by
repeating it you would not be injuring anyone and would not
object to having it published over your signature?
If what you might otherwise say will not bear this test, let it
alone. Let the subject drop right there. If you won't sign it, you
are suspicious of it, so DON'T SAY IT.

ARSONIST DESERVE NO SYMPATHY
The fire loss for 1927 has been estimated at $560,548,624, the
highest in the history of the country.
Estimates of the proportion of set fires range from 15 to 75
per cent, but it will be conceded that 33 per cent is a conservative
figure, giving us a total of $186,849,540 as the loss due to incend
iarism and arson during 1927.
When business is good, fire losses are low, when business is
bad, fire losses are high.
For some reason the American people persist. in winking at
the most usual form of arson, which is the fraud fire, the burning
3i property to obtain the insurance. People do not seem to under
tand that when insurance companies unwittingly pay a false
claim, the ultimate losers are not the underwriters, but every
insured property.
There is entirily too much sympathy wasted on the individual
vho can put it over the insurance companies when in reality he
is putting it over nobody but his sympathetic friends and neigh
bors. Arson is one of the most difficult of crimes to prove because
the fires destroy the evidence. Fire insurance agents are closely
associated -with the crime of arson from the very nature of their
business. The agent is peculiarly well placed to render service, both
to the insurance companies and to the public, in the suppression
of arson.
There is no greater service the agents can perform in the
nterests of the business they represent and of the public than to
themselves thourouerhly as to whether moral hazard
exists before placing insurance and to impress upon the people of
their communities the heinousness of the crime of arson.
The public must be made to realize that arson is the crime on
the calendar which is practically always committed in cold blood.
Arson is cruel, cold and planned always in advance and carried out
by stealth. There are about it no extenuating circomstances to
recommend lenience in dealing with. There is not the smallest
loophole for sentimental forgiveness. The arsonist strikes like the
viper, without warning and without heed to human life, and he.
must be stamped out as is the snake he emulates. It frequently
causes the death of those who are entirely innocent and in many
instances spreads destruction to neighboring property.
