A UNIFORM MOTOR LAW
The Federal Department of Commerce announces that the
first step has .been taken to give to all the states a uniform code
for the. regulation of automobile traffic.
The proposed rules have been drafted by a committee named
for the purpose, and will be submitted to the national conference
on street and highway safety.
If approved, they will be passed on to the various states fof
enactment into law. It is not proposed to force upon the states a
federal statute for the governing of traffic, but merely to suggest
for their consideration a compilation of rules and regulations
that have been approved by a national conference of stàte rep
resentatives. -
Such a plan, as we see it, would simplify traffic throughout
the country and do away entirely with the confusion that obtains
at present.
The driver of an automobile is never sure of himself after he
crosses the line separating one state from another.
Any Bridgeport man who drives an automobile does, or
should, know the regulations in this city, but if he were to ob
serve them while passing through another municipality he might
be haled before a court on the charge of violating the traffic rules
of that town.
It is even said on good authority that some of the smaller
towns have passed ordinances merely as traps for strangers driv
ing over the highways that pass through these towns. No doubt
thousands of law-observing men each year are forced to pay fines
in this way, when they are guilty of no real offense whatever.
Save in minor matters, we know of no reason why a traffic
law that would be an aid to safety in New York shouldn't pro
mote safety in San Francisco.
The advantage of uniform motor laws is obvious.
WHY NOT GO TO CHURCH?
Churches of all denominations and faiths appear to be in
agreement on one thing, and that is that the period just preceding
Easter is a favorable time in which to arouse their members to a
renewed interest in religious work. Locally, as well as in other
cities, special effort is being made to reach indifferent and lax
members, as well as the unchurched. We offer no apology for
commenting favorably upon the effort. Perhaps we do it from
a selfish motive. We believe the community will be better the
other six days in the week if the churches are filled on Sunday.
Many Bridgeport citizens never darken the doors of any church.
Some of them are members of churches, too. They may have
become dissatisfied because the workers and leaders were not
perfect. If they were perfect, the millenium would be here. Some
remain away because they do not like the preacher. In such case,
there are other churches to choose from. Some stay away, we
suppose, because the minister would hit their weak tendencies in
his sermon. The chances are he is talking at his deacons. Attend
ance at church services, at least occasionally, rs a small thing to
do, in a country which calls itself enlightened and civilized. Let's
go to church, Sunday.
