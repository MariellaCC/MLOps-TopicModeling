THANKSGIVING DAY
The advent of Thansgiving Day serves to remind many of us
of the wonderous changes which time has wrought. It brings to
our minds a recollection of things which took place in days which
are gone.
We think òf the picture of the first Thanksgiving, of the
deeds and dress of those who in the early days- of this country
expressed their thankfulness 'for all that had been recc-led. And
then we think of the changes.
Tippets and mittens are things of a bygone period. They
Lang on the same nail in memory's closet as the high tock, men's
shawls and daguerreotypes. Memory brings to mind the mittens
which were knit at home and which were long ago sacrificed to
the factory-made glove. -; Then, too, there was the boot jack,un
known today with our ready-made shoes.
Also the coming of-this holiday brings back to -our minds
other Thanksgiving days, the dear dead days which were mile
stones on our road of life; and with these days come once more
memories, unutterable and incommunicable. There come to each of
us visitations from memory, land, that land which is the most real
of all lands. x .
But whether we think of the Thanksgiving days which have
passed, those of the pioneer days of our ancestors and those of
our earlier life, or of the holiday as we are celebrating it now
we will find trough it all one idea which remains the same and,
that relates to the purpose of the day.- ,
Thanksgiving still remains a day on which we give thanks
for the things which we have received. Thanksgiving Day was
first organized for the purpose of giving1 thanks to God for the
blessing which He has showered down upon us. Today we observe
the holiday in the same spirit.
We find ourselves thankful for the good things which we
have received during the past year. But greatest of all we find
ourselves thankful for the privilege of living in the only nation
which observes this holiday, a nation which has; been more
greatly blessed with the good things of the world than the major
ity of the other countries. .:
This past year has been none to prosperous in the United
States, but in comparison with other nations there are few who
have really suffered here. We today are living in the most favor
ed nation of the world, and for that as well as all the other
blessings which we have received we should be deeply thankful.

after year has had any noticeable effect in the formation of a
commendable habit this is the time when evidencev of early

"IT ISNT
Almost since the discovery of firearms "It isn't loaded" has
taken its toll in accidental deaths, and, in spite of all the warning
propaganda, still continues.
Only this morning I read of a boy who came home from a
vacation in the West, and, in showing his mother his automatic
pistol, accidentally shot and killed her. Imagine that terrible feel
ing of tragedy of having killed one's own mother. Imagine having
the thought follow you through life that trough your own criminal
carelessness y6u have deprived yourself of one of the greatest
gifts on earth.
"It isn't loaded", like "Stop, Look and Listen", forces human
ity to pay a stiff price for negligence, and yet it seems that no
two faults of humans have been given greater and more forceful
publicity. ''-- ;
WORK AND
Work is a tonic for ambition, a specific for worry, and a great
radicator of sorrow. But it is like other medicine. It is an acquired
taste. Some men learn to take work in large doses every day and
smack their lips over it; while others take it as if it were
quinine, and their yells can be heard for bloocks hours before
quitting time. The man who likes work is sure to be happy most
of the time ; but the man who has sharp, shooting pains whenever
he is in the vicinity of a job is doomed to trouble and a close
acquaitance with the constable and the bill collector.
