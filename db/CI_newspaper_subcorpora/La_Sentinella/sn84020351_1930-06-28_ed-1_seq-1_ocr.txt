KNOWLEDGE VS. WISDOM
By Dr. Joseph Pantaleone
Of what value is a man's knowledge if he doesn't know how
to use it to the best possible advantage? Of what use is a man's
knowledge if his philosophy of life renders his existence a wretched
one? Why should I have any more respect for a man who has
stuffed his head full of facts and who shows no other sign of
intelligence than that of being a pure memory machine; I say,
why should I have any more respect for him than I have for an
ordinary five-dollar book?. The chances are that the book will have
the material far better organized than he can ever hope to have
himself. When shall we stop worshiping brute memory and
putting premium on it in our educational system, . when what
really counts in life is not the number of facts we have succeeded
in storing into our cranium, but rather the way we use our know
ledge and the amount of sound philosophy, initiative, originality,
courage and idealism we put in our ordinary dealings? The
successful man is not necessarily the one who knows most, but he
it? the one who thinks most and has the ability and the courage
to transform his convictions into action.
The writers of all ages have delighted in contrasting knowledge
with wisdom and wisdom is always favored above knowledge. But
while knowledge is easy to acquire, since any fool can with a little
perseverance learn like a parrot countless facts, wisdom on thje
other hand, is difficult art to learn and it is not given to every one
to be so gifted. Wisdom involves a deep intuitive knowledge
it is the supreme art of knowing how to get the most out of life.
To have wisdom means to have infinite tact and resourcefulness,
to be keenly aware of our numberless relations to all the rest of
the world and to have the ability to use these to our best advan
tage. What becomes of mere memory when compared to this
stupendous art? Imparting wisdom ought to be the primary
object of true education, not the development of memory alone.
It is wisdom to love all truth, beauty, and goodness for their
own sake but how far have we succeeded in imparting this in
our schools, college and universities- '
A certain amount of wisdom can be taught in the schools and
I believe it can be developed by every man by systematic thinking
on life's problems and this is certainly .infinitely more valuable -than
the possession of vast stores of encyclopaedic knowledge.
INDEPENDENCE DAY
There is no day in the year that is. more pre-eminently
American than Independence Day, and there is no anniversary
dealing with the country that has more significance than the
Foutrh of July. It is observed in every state in the Union, and is
not only confined to America, but wherever you see citizens of the
United States on the Fourth of July you will get a buzz of
American enthusiasm. :
We can find no preMc sentiments in deahngSvith:4this dayj
than the wards uttered by Daniel Webster in his address of July
4, 1851, at the lying of the cornerstone of the new wing of the
capitol at Washington, when he said:
"On the fourth of July, 1776, the representatives of the
United States of America, in Congress assembled, declared that
these Colonies are, and ought to be, free and independent states.
This declaration, made by most patriotic and resolute men,
trusting in the justice of their cause and the protection of Heaven,
and yet not without deep solicitude and anxiety, has now
stood for seventy-five years. It was sealed in blood. It has met
dangers and overcome them. It has had. detractions, and abashed
them all. It has has enemies, and conquered them. It has had
doubting friends, but it has cleared all doubts away; and now,
today, raising its august form higher than the clouds, twenty
millions of people contemplate it with hallowed love, and the
world beholds it, and the consequences that have followed from
it, with profound admiration.
"This anniversary animates and gladdens all American hearts.
On the other days of the year we may be party men, indulging in
controversies more or less important to the public good. - We may
have likes and dislikes, and we may maintain our political differ
ences, often with warm and sometimes with angry feelings. But
today we are all Americans all; and all nothing but Americans.
"As the great luminary over our heads," dissapating fogs and
mist, now cheers the whole atmosphere, so do the associations
connected with this day disperse all sullen -and cloudy weather
in the minds and feelings of true Americans. Every man's heart
sweels within him. Every man's port and bearing become some
what more proud and lofty as he remembers that seventy-five
years have rolled away, and that the great inheritance of Liberty
is still his, his, undiminished and unimpaired; his, in all its
original glory; his to enjoy; his to protect; his to transmit to
future generations."
A HISTORICAL NOTE ABOUT INDEPENDENCE DAY
There is an interesting story connected with the incident of
John Hancock's signing of the Declaration of Independence, which
explains the frequent reference to one's signature as one's "John
Hancock". x .
What the colonists were doing, of course, was starting a
revolution, and some of the brethren were fearful, lest things
might not work out so well. So the cautious ones suggested that
perhaps it might better to sign fictitious names or at least sign
illegibly so that, in case the document were seized, it would provide
no directory of the plotter.
Apparently this disgusted Mr. Hancock, who stepped up and
boldly taking the pen, or quill, inscribed his name in unusually
large and legible letters, at the same time making remarks to the
effect that he didn't care if King George himself saw his name
there. His action is reported to have quelled all talk of pussy
footing. We could hardly hold this sacred national document in the
reverence which we now do, if we knew that the signatures were
cowardly. And we are indebted, in all probability, to the fearless
ness and straightforwardness of the statesmen for the fact that
the Declaration of Independence is genuine signature and all.
UNEMPLOYMENT AND PATRONIZING HOME FOLKS
We have heard much in recent months about boosting our
home merchants and not patronizing the chain stores. This is
logical doctrine and we believe in patronizing our home merchants
and home folks. However lets apply the same thing in another
case. You want us to spend our home money with home merchants,
yet, if you have a job to let, you go beyond the bounds of our
towns to find the labor and right in your own home town there
are hundreds of home laborers that are well qualified to do the
work. If you have a school to build, a church to erect, a house
to paint, or need a bookeeper or clerk for your store, you go to
the other place to secure the help you want and ignore home
talent. The only difference in this, is that the money people
spend in our chain stores perhaps goes directly to Wall Street or
ome other distant town, while the wages you pay to outside labor
goes into other districts Saturday noon and is perhaps spent in a
chain store that night or given to a merchant in some other
town that has no interest whatever in your town.
