HOOVER'S ACCEPTANCE SPEECH j
The outstanding characteristic of Herber Hoover's acceptance
speech says Mark Sullivan, foremost political writer of America
compared with those of former candidates is unity of theme.
The underlying subject is the next coming phase of American
prosperity. The prosperity itself he is very confident of. With
continuation of present policies, he takes the prosperity for.
granted.
What he speculates about is what social and spiritual use the
make of our material abundance and economic security. He vis
ualizes America not as at a cross-roads, or a turn, but rather at a
point where the road broadens out to more widely . distributed
well-being. With the mechanism for producing material abundance
running smoothly, he devotes himself to new social advances .which
he is satisfied can be built upon it.
With calm confidence he assumes the solution of society's old
est problem. "Given a chance," he says, "to go forward with the
policies of the last eight years, we shall soon with the help of God
be in sight of the day when poverty will be banished from this
nation." That, in Mr. Hoover's case, is not a mere homiletic aspir
ation but an engineer'r estimate. IJis more distant goal, and the
central theme at once of his speech and also of his own nature, is
racial progress. It is to be accomplished through attention to the
race's young. "Racial progress" he says, "marches upon the feet
of healthy and instructed children."
More specifically the goal he holds up is that every child in
America shall be born and live under sounding conditions, shall
have full opportunity of education from the beginning to the end
f our institutions, shall be free from injurious labor and shall
have every stimulation to accomplish the fullest of its capacities.
The suffusing keynote of Mr. Hoover's speech is new among
acceptances. Like all previous ones, it reflects the period in which
it is made. Mr. Coolidge's keynote in 1924 was economy. Warren
G. Harding's in 1920 were "return to normalcy" and foreign rela
tion. Charles E. Hughes acceptance in 1916 dwelt upon our rela
tion to the war then raging, including a call to preparedness.
William H. Taf t's acceptance speech in 1908 praised Theodore
Roosevelt's program of social reform and in 1912 criticized it as
going too far. Colonel Roosevelt's acceptance in 1904 was one of
the briefest, being chiefly a defiance of the Democrats for what
he seemed to regard as their uncalled for proposal to deny him
a second term. William McKinley's keynote in 1900 dealt almost .
wholly with pur then new expansion in the West Indies and The
Philippines, an issue thén all-consuming but destined quickly to
pass. If Mr. Hoover's version is even apporximately correct, Ame
rica has an extraordinary period ahead of it.
AN AMERICAN LOOKS OUT ON HIS WORLD
It is but a little over 150 years since a handful of "men end
women embarked on the great adventure which men call- America.
What a miracle we have wrought !
More than a hundred million people we are and there is peace
and plahty in the land. Factories belch their fire and shining
slaves of steeL march out to do our. bidding. Beautiful homes dot
the countryside. Theaters of drama, of photoplays, almost blot out
the sun. The smallest restaurant on Main Street presents a
gastronomic display sufficient to arouse the envy of the bravest
gourmand. Lower berths are as hard to obtain as tickets to a
Broadway success. Hotel rooms are at a premium. Golf links are
as common as a sidewalk pumps. Savings banks groan under thè
weight of the gold fhey hold. Two prizefighters command enough
money in a single evening to wipe out the course of leprosy from
the world. Industry has passed into the hands of the people,
trough millions of owners of stocks and bonds. By the grace of
God we have more leisure, more comfort, more security than any
people in the world's history have ever had. -
But an American looks out on his world today and in the face
of this, and in spite of this, all is not well.
He listens to the voices which come to us from across the seas
and he hears a murmur, a grumbling of criticism and even of hate.
He looks into the homes and he beholds a restlessness, an
irritability and a discontent that is strange in the face of all
these things.
He reads the records of divorce courts and criminal courts,
and is shocked by figures that are ill and foreboding, in the story
they tell.
He hears parents despair of their children. He hears children
ridicule their parents and their ideals.
He gazes out on this people which only a hundred and fifty
years ago gave its blood for freedom and finds an indifference
to government and citizenship which is startling.
And he wonders.
Why, in the face of all this prosperity, do men and women
recall so fondly, other days? Why do so many of us rush away
from this panorame of plenty and seek out a blade of grass the
shelter of a tree to think of plenty and to wonder upon it all ?
What is this strange uneasiness which keeps knocking at our
hearts? Has something gone out of our lives? One wonders. Is
the price we are paying too great? Or have we wandered into
strange lands?
Perhaps it is time to ask a question. - i
How much of all this is America? - ''
IT TAKES AT LEAST ONE FOOL TO MAKE A WRECK
There are automobile wrecks caused by defective machinery
and defective roads, but nine times out of ten the fact of a wreck
proves the existenc of a fool.
And when two cars wreck it is a safe bet 99 in a hundred
that . one of the drivers, or both, violated rule or some rule of
common sense.
Almost every driver thinks he is a good one, and yet not one
driver in 10 has wit enough or is prudent enough to deserve the
use of public highways. If the other nine escape having wrecks
it is because of th mercy of God.
You may drive at a rate of 60 miles and yet be. a safe driver.
.But you aren t a sate driver if you pass slower cars at a curve.
You aren't a good driver if you pass other cars at the crest of the
hill where you cannot see the highway ahead. And you aren't a
safe driver if you take chances.
In short, the only driver who should be trusted on the high
way is the one who keeps his car under absolute control at all
times, whether making 10 miles an hour or 60; who never, at any
speed, gets so close to the car in front of him that he could not
avoid a collision if it should stop without warning; who never
approaches a crossing without being certain that the way is clear;
who never, in short, gets into a position or condition where he
could not avoid a wreck if he should meet an idiot.' -
It is not enough merely to drive safely. It is not. enough to be
safe on condition that other drivers obey the rules. The good
driver must assume that he may at any time meet a poor driver,
a fool or a drunk man, and must be in a position to save himself
regardless of the other driver's folly. v
