ITALIAN IN HIGH SCHOOLS
(from The Bridgeport Telegram)
The compromise by which the proposed teaching-
of Italian language in the high schools of
Bridgeport is to be shunted off into the night
schools is an evasion of the issue rather than a
settlement of it. Those who asked for the arrang
ement desired that all high school pupils be given
an opportunity to learn the Italian language as an
alternative to one of the other modern languages
now being- taught. No high school pupil, after
doing his day's work at either Central or Harding-
High school, is going back to school after supper
and put in an evening studying Italian.
If Bridgeport with all its teaching facilities has
nobody on the staff of either of the high schools
who can teach Italian in connection with some
other modern language and so handle classes in
both, then the .Board of Education ought to say so
frankly, and make provisions for engaging such
an instructor for the beginning of next school
term.
The big eastern universities have long since
rated Italian as one of the acceptable subjects in
the modern language groups in college entrance
examinations. In other words, study of this lan
guage is a step toward college, for those who are
bent in that direction. On the other hand there
are many high school pupils who would welcome
a chance to get acquainted with Italian, whether
for reasons of sentiment, for music study, or
because of the lingual beauties of the speech
itself.
A preliminary survey in Brilgeport disclosed
141 pupils now in one or the other of the Bridge
port's two high schools who wanted this language
and the chances are that the number would in
crease, rather than diminish, as the years went
on. It certainly should be possible, to make pro
vision for a teacher of Italian in the High schools
beginning with the September term.
The Telegram expresses our own opinion in the matter in
plain and convincing terms. The Italian element in Bridgeport has
telt a keen disappointment over the announcement that the
Italian course is to be shifted to the evening- classes rather than
to be included as a regular course at day sessions along with other
modern languages, so much so that several Italian . organizations
had already voted funds for establishing prizes to students of
Italian at closing of sessions.
The interest of the Italian element in the matter is that the
study of the Italian language in our schools will furnish an effect
ive link between the people of Italian origin and the general
public trough better acquaintance of Italy and its people.
We confess that we cannot see clear in the hesitancy of the
Board of Education in establishing an Italian course, following the
large demand by high school students for an Italian course and
we sincerely hope that no prejudice has been insected in the
matter, though it looks very much so, and confidentely await
further action rescinding the Evening Class farce and ordering
the study of Italian language as a regular course of the Central
and Harding High Schools.

CHECK UP ON YOUR HAPPINESS
If you are happy, just why are you happy?
Check up !
Well, for one thing you have got good health.
The clean streets, the water works, the health department
and pleasant working conditions are partly responsible.
Ever think that you made them all possible that you con
tributed your share?
Part of every dollar that you spend at home has found its
way back to these places and helped guard your health and the
health of your dear ones.
. You are happy because you have a job or a business. Be
thankful that others like yourself have made your livelihood
possible by spending their- dollar at home where part of them
eventually reach you. . - "
You are happy because you are comfortable. That piano, the.
dress your wife bought last week, the dinner that's waiting for
you this evening were all paid for out of money others here in
your home town paid you.
They migth have paid it to someone a hundred or a thousand
miles away, and you migth have lacked work and the comforts
and necessities of life. But they elected to spend it with you be
cause they knew they were keeping the money at home. Are you
spending and saving it here, too? Are you giving them a chance
to get some of it back, too?
You should : for your own sake!
For nowhere else can you get better value, whether it's a
radio, a dress, a necktie, a automobile, a dining room suite or a
home boy or girl looking for a job.
You can't really be happy unless you are making other people
happy at same time.
Check up on your happiness. Your neighbors, friends and
business acquaintances, are responsible for it. They spend their
money at home, where part of it, no matter how small or large, .
comes back to you.
Do as much for them. Save and spend at home.

THE PRICE 0F CARELESSNESS
The long expected has has happened. Owners of private pass
enger cars, beginning December 12, will have te pay an increased
rate for public liability insurance. The raise for the whole
averages about 16 per cent. For some years past, the rate on this
type of insurance has been going down steadily.
The experience data on which the new rates are based, covers
the period from 1923 to 1927, inclusive. This data shows the
downward trend of accidents stopped and turned abruptly upward
in 1925. In 1926, the insurance companies as a whole suffered an
underwriting loss, and the available data for 1927 shows that the
upward trend of losses is continuing.
The new rates were adopted after long and careful study and
the principal causes which forced upward revision of rates are:
1. More powerful automobiles and greater speed.
2. Increased mileage of improved highways and greater use
of automobiles.
3. Increasing frequency of claims.
4. Growing tendency on part of injured persons to demand
larger damages. . .
5. Marked tendency on part of juries to award larger
damages
Every dav the newspapers recount automobile accidents
where not only one but numbers of persons are injured and killed
by one collision. Accident prevention work has been carried on by
railroads, insurance companies, public utilities, private industries,
cities and states. If it had not been for this work, rates would
have shown a greater increase.
Commenting on the situation, Albert W. Whitney, Acting
General Manager of the National Bureau of Casualty and Security-
Underwriters, says that praiseworthy results have been accom
plished through traffic regulation, strict examination of requir
ements for drivers, safety campaingns among both drivers and
pedestrians, revocation of the driving licenses of operators and
penalties imposed by courts on careless and reckless operators.
"There is much work yet to be done in the field of accident
prevention and the insurance companies are among the most
enthusiastic in hoping that the near future will bring forth
resutls which will justify a downward revision of rates.
In the meantime, the situation is largely up to the public. It
can cooperate to reduce accidents or it can destroy safety measures
;.nd in addition to injury and death, it can pay increased insurance
rates. The price of carelessness comes high, with or without insur
ance.
