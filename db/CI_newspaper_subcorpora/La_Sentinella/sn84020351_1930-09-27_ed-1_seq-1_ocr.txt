Within the last few months prices have taken a terrific fall
due to the depression, and the buying public, if it is in a position
to make purchases, should do so now. As we .scan the market,
pages of various newspapers we are agreeable surprised witfi the

THE MOUNTING TOLL OF TAXATION
One sixth of this contry's total earning capacity, is our toll
lo taxation, according to Merle Thorpe in Nation's Business. Our
tax bill, he says, is nearly 13 billion dollars yearly, "while the
yearly earnings of all of us men, women and children is 80
billion. Mr. Thorpe likewise points out that one person out of
every eleven who are gainfully employed, is a public employee,
while not so many years ago it required only one out of every 22
persons to perform the services we demanded of Government.
Much of this taxation bill is due to the more or less general
theory that anything furnishad by the Government is free.
Therefore business men continue to boost for more Governmental
activities, only to find that the old economic law holds good, and
eventually we pay for all we get.
EDITORS ARE HUMAN
When a doubtful situation arises in a town it is quite the
fashion to remark that "the paper ought to say something about
that."
The average citizen feels quite certain he could run a news
paper better than the editor does, and if he were publisher -of
that sheet he'd show 'em, you bet.
As a matter of fact, if he has horse sense he would do just
as the editor does, put the soft pedal on family rows, church
squabbles, scandals not involving principles, and such matters of
minor importance as will adjust themselves with the passing of
time.
The newspaper critic ought not to expect the newspaper man
to advance or attack any proposition which he himself hasn't the
courage to support or assail over his own signature. .
The editor is willing, even eager, to push any project in the
public interest. But he is not willing to pull chestnuts out of the
fire for individual or minorities.
TO FULL TO MOVE
The cry of suffering America would appear to be "Give Us
Something New To Sell That Everybody Wants To Buy" and
preferably something to play with ! We are burdened not with
poverty but a plethora of prosperity. Saturation is the cause. We
do not wear enough cotton or leather ; we do not eat enough sugar
or wheat. There is not room enough in the roads for any more
motor cars and their sale lags in consequence. Congestion is at
the bottom of it all. This prevails in every line. There is even
too much money to be put in profitable employment. In short, the
nation has been playing the greedy boy until it is stuffed too full
to move.
